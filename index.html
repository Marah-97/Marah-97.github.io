The Dataset

San Francisco Police Reports

This analysis draws on two public datasets published by the San Francisco Police Department on the SF Open Data Portal: historical incident reports from 2003 to May 2018, and incident reports from 2018 to the present.

After merging and standardizing the two datasets, the combined record spans 22 years of reported crime across all police districts in the city. Our focused subset covers police reports from 2003 to the present day.

From this combined dataset, we selected nine Personal Focus Crimes to capture a broad variety of crime types — violent, property-based, and enforcement-driven — so that contrasting trends across categories can reveal something meaningful about how social and policy changes shape the city's crime landscape over time.

Temporal Overview

We visualize the total number of incidents per year across the nine selected focus categories from 2003 to 2025, excluding the current year because the data is incomplete.

Figure 1.1. Annual incident counts for all nine focus crimes from 2003 to 2025. Each line represents one crime category.

Figure 1.1 gives insight into how different social, political and societal changes impact the rates of recorded crimes. We explore different explanations to the most significant trends and changes.

1) Drop in assault during COVID

There is a noticeable drop in recorded assault offences around 2019–2020. During this time, restrictions, the public mood and fear of contamination, limited nightlife, big gatherings and overall face-to-face interaction. This meant fewer opportunities for interpersonal violence, which is clearly reflected in the data.

2) Surge in burglaries and vehicle theft during COVID

During the COVID years (2019–2021), the data shows an opposite, but just as noticeable, change — a surge in recorded vehicle thefts and burglaries. Empty buildings, reduced street activity, and longer periods of parked vehicles created more opportunities for property crime. Additionally, COVID was a time of increased economic stress, which likely also contributed to this rise.

3) Recent rise in drug offences

Drug offences declined steadily for most of the timeline until around the pandemic period in 2019. This long-term decrease can be explained by a general change in attitude towards drug use, where enforcement and arrest were reduced and replaced by diversion and treatment. By 2024, however, the data shows a surge in drug offences, rising above pre-pandemic levels. In 2024, the mayor signed legislation allowing police to arrest drug users and send them to reset centers, reflecting a change in policy and enforcement focus in response to public pressure tied to highly visible drug markets.

Assignment 1.2

Crime Profiles by District

We calculate the conditional crime profile ratios for each police district. This shows which types of crimes are more or less common in each district compared to the city average.

🗺️ Figure 2 — Crime profile ratios by police district Replace with: <img src="plot1.2.png">

Figure 1.2. Heatmap showing crime profile ratios r(crime, district) for all SF police districts. Green (values > 1) = over-represented; red (values < 1) = under-represented relative to the city-wide average. Rows sorted by each district's dominant crime type.

The heatmap shows the ratio r(crime, district) = P(crime | district) / P(crime) for each combination of police district and crime category. A value of 1.0 means the crime occurs at exactly the city-wide average rate; values above 1 indicate over-representation and values below 1 indicate under-representation.

Tenderloin

The most striking district in the entire heatmap. It has a drug offences ratio of 3.28 — meaning drug crime is more than three times as common there compared to the city average. At the same time, vehicle theft is heavily under-represented (0.25), which makes sense given that the Tenderloin is a dense, pedestrian-heavy neighborhood with fewer privately parked vehicles. The Tenderloin has long been the center of San Francisco's open-air drug market, and this data confirms that pattern clearly.

Out of SF

Stands out for vehicle theft (2.04) and sex offences (1.86), while having very low burglary (0.27) and assault (0.60). The high vehicle theft ratio likely reflects that incidents near highways or city boundaries — where cars are more vulnerable and less monitored — are logged under this category.

Richmond and Taraval

Both show elevated burglary ratios (1.42 and 1.24 respectively) and low drug offences (0.30 and 0.31). These are quieter, residential western neighborhoods with more houses and shops to burglarize, but far less street-level drug activity than central districts.

Central and Northern

Show relatively balanced profiles close to 1.0 across most categories, suggesting they reflect the city-wide average reasonably well — unsurprising given their central location covering mixed-use tourist and commercial areas.

Overall, the heatmap reveals that crime in San Francisco is far from uniformly distributed. Each district has a distinct signature shaped by its geography, land use, and socioeconomic conditions.

Visualizing Distributions

We recreate classic plot types from DAOST Chapter 2 using our crime data, examining time recording patterns, geographic distributions, and time-of-day activity.

Part A — Jitter Plot

To investigate how incident times are recorded in the dataset, we selected assault as our focus crime and filtered incidents occurring between 13:00–14:00 over a three-month period (January–March 2015).

⠿ Figure 3 — Jitter plot: assault 13:00–14:00 (Jan–Mar 2015) Replace with: <img src="plot1.3.png">

Figure 1.3. Jitter plot showing recorded times for assault incidents between 13:00–14:00 (January–March 2015). Each dot represents a single recorded incident. The pronounced spike at minute 0 and smaller cluster at minute 30 indicate systematic rounding of incident times to the nearest hour or half-hour.

In Figure 1.3, each dot represents a single recorded incident, with the x-axis showing the minute within the hour and the points spread out vertically to avoid overlap. The big cluster at minute 0 shows more incidents logged exactly on the hour than at any other minute. A smaller cluster sits at minute 30 as well.

The most likely reason is that officers round the time when writing up their report rather than recording the exact minute. What this means in practice is that the recorded times can't be taken at face value — any analysis that tries to pinpoint exact minutes would be reading too much into the data.

Part B — QQ Plot

To examine the geographic distribution of crime across the city, we created QQ plots comparing the latitude distribution of two focus crimes — assault and vehicle theft — against a normal (Gaussian) reference distribution.

〰 Figure 4 — QQ plots of latitude distributions Replace with: <img src="plot1.4.png">

Figure 1.4. Q-Q plots comparing the latitude distribution of assault and vehicle theft incidents against a theoretical normal distribution. The red diagonal line represents a perfect normal distribution.

In Figure 1.4, we see that both plots follow the reference line reasonably well in the middle, suggesting that the bulk of incidents are distributed fairly symmetrically across the north-south span of San Francisco. However, both plots show clear deviations at the tails — points flatten out and cluster around specific latitude values, indicating that incidents concentrate in particular areas rather than spreading evenly.

The S-shaped curve visible in both plots is a classic sign of a distribution with lighter tails than a normal distribution. We notice that both assault and vehicle theft show almost identical QQ plot shapes, suggesting their geographic distributions are very similar despite being very different types of crime.

Part C — Box Plots of Time-of-Day

To examine when different crimes occur throughout the day, we extracted the hour of each incident and created side-by-side box plots for all nine focus crimes.

◫ Figure 5 — Time-of-day distributions by crime type Replace with: <img src="plot1.5.png">

Figure 1.5. Box plots showing the time-of-day distribution for each of the nine focus crimes. The box spans the interquartile range, the line inside is the median, and the whiskers extend to min/max recorded times.

In Figure 1.5 we see how all nine crime categories are distributed across the day. All of them occur predominantly between 06:00 and 18:00, suggesting that the majority of reported crimes in San Francisco happen during daylight hours regardless of crime type.

Drug offences show the tightest distribution, with a median at around 15:00 and a box spanning roughly 12:00 to 18:00. This narrow spread indicates that drug-related incidents are strongly concentrated in the afternoon.

Sex offence, on the other hand, has a median around 12:00 but the widest box of all categories, stretching from roughly 03:00 to 18:00, reflecting that sex offences are recorded across almost every hour of the day.

Key limitation: The midnight wrap-around problem. Crimes like assault and sex offence genuinely occur at night, but because the clock resets at 00:00, incidents at 23:00 and 01:00 are treated as being 22 hours apart rather than 2. A circular time representation or 24-hour polar plot would handle this more honestly.

Spatial Power Law

We look at the spatial distribution of crime to uncover whether crime is evenly distributed throughout the city, or concentrated in only a few hotspots. We define a grid overlay, count crimes in each cell, and analyse the frequency distribution.

We first identified the most common crime from our focus group: assault — the highest-incident category. We then defined a 100m × 100m grid to cover the entire area of San Francisco and counted assault incidents per cell.

Figure 1.6. The frequency distribution N(k) plotted against number of crimes k. Left: linear axes. Right: log-log axes. The straight line on the log-log plot reveals a power law distribution.

Power Law Distribution

Figure 1.6 provides strong visual evidence for a power law distribution:

On linear scales, the distribution is crushed along the axes. Most values lie in the low end, meanwhile there is a heavy tail with only very few values over larger counts.

On log-log scales, the power law distribution becomes clear. Values fall approximately along a straight line over a range of counts.

Implications for neighborhood-level statistics

Assault incidents follow a power-law distribution and are spatially concentrated in a small number of grid cells. Because crime is heavily concentrated in a few hotspots, the average number of crimes per grid cell becomes misleading.

The gap between the mean (22) and the median (6) tells us that most grid cells have assault counts well below the average. The mean is inflated by a small number of high-crime hotspots. In fact, the grid with the most crimes has 3,032 recordings.

When we average the number of crimes on a neighborhood level, it has a misleading effect: the city appears moderately dangerous on average, but in reality it consists of mostly safe neighborhoods with only a few areas of very high crime concentration.

Regression and Correlation

The most correlated pair is vehicle theft vs vandalism with R² ≈ 0.848. This suggests a strong shared weekly rhythm, which makes sense because both are property-related crimes often tied to similar conditions: traffic patterns, parking behavior, and nighttime/weekend activity.

The least correlated pair is disorderly conduct vs robbery with R² ≈ 0.004, indicating almost no linear alignment in their weekly patterns. Disorderly conduct is often linked to local street-level disturbances and enforcement context, while robbery can be driven by different situational opportunities and victim/offender dynamics.

Overall, the regression matrix shows that some crime categories appear to move together across the week, while others are effectively out of sync. This supports the idea that different crime types are generated by different social factors and local conditions, even within the same city and time period.
