Introduction

The Dataset

This analysis draws on two public datasets published by the San Francisco Police Department on the SF Open Data Portal: historical incident reports from 2003 to May 2018, and incident reports from 2018 to the present. After merging and standardizing the two datasets, the combined record spans 22 years of reported crime across all police districts in the city.

From this combined dataset, we selected seven Personal Focus Crimes:

Assault Vehicle Theft Drug Offences Burglary Robbery Suspicious Sex Offence

These crimes were chosen to capture a broad variety of crime types ‚Äî violent, property-based, and enforcement-driven ‚Äî so that contrasting trends across categories can reveal something meaningful about how social and policy changes shape the city's crime landscape over time.

Looking across the full 22-year period, the most striking signal is the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic around 2020, which pushed different crime types in sharply opposite directions.

Assault dropped noticeably in 2020. COVID restrictions limited nightlife, public gatherings, and face-to-face interactions, removing many of the everyday situations that lead to interpersonal violence.

Burglary and vehicle theft moved in the opposite direction. Empty buildings, reduced street activity, and the economic stress of the pandemic all created more opportunities for property crime. This divergence ‚Äî violent crime down, property crime up ‚Äî was a pattern documented in many cities during the pandemic period.

Drug offences tell a longer story. They declined steadily from around 2009 until the pandemic, driven by a shift toward treatment over arrest for low-level possession. By 2024, however, recorded drug offences rose above pre-pandemic levels as San Francisco increased enforcement in response to the visible fentanyl crisis. In 2024, the mayor signed legislation allowing police to transport drug users directly to treatment facilities.

Robbery shows a steady long-term decline across the entire period. The shift toward cashless payments, widespread smartphone tracking, and increased surveillance have reduced both the opportunity and payoff of robbery over two decades ‚Äî a trend that continued even during periods of wider social disruption.

Figure 2. Heatmap of crime profile ratios r(crime, district) for all 11 SF police districts. Green = over-represented relative to city average (ratio > 1); orange = under-represented (ratio < 1). A value of 1.0 is exactly the city-wide average. Rows are sorted by each district's dominant crime type.

Each cell shows how common a crime is in a given district relative to the city-wide average. A value of 1.0 means exactly average; 2.0 means twice as common; 0.5 means half as common. This normalization allows fair comparison across districts of very different sizes and total crime volumes.

The Tenderloin stands out immediately, with a drug offences ratio of 3.28 ‚Äî meaning drug crime is more than three times as common there compared to the city average. At the same time, vehicle theft is heavily under-represented (0.25), which makes sense given that the Tenderloin is a dense, pedestrian-heavy neighborhood with few privately parked vehicles. The city declared a state of emergency in the Tenderloin in late 2021 in response to overdose deaths and open-air drug markets ‚Äî and this data confirms that picture with striking clarity.

Out of SF shows elevated vehicle theft (2.04) and sex offences (1.86), likely reflecting incidents near highways or city boundaries where cars are more vulnerable. Richmond and Taraval both have elevated burglary ratios (1.42 and 1.24) ‚Äî quieter residential western neighborhoods with more homes and shops to burglarize but far less street-level drug activity. Central and Northern show profiles close to 1.0 across most categories, reflecting their mixed tourist and commercial character.
